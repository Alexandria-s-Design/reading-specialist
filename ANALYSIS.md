## Part 1: Photo Material Analysis (16 Images)

These 16 photographs capture pages from Jan Richardson's *The Next Step Forward in Guided Reading* (Scholastic, 2016), specifically **Chapter 4: Early Guided Reading** and supplemental **Early Transitional** lesson materials. Below is a detailed breakdown of what each image contains and the instructional procedures described.

### Image 01 — Day 1: What's Missing, Mix & Fix, Table Writing, Whiteboards
**Page: Explanations of Procedures (Day 1)**

Four word study activities for building sight word knowledge:

- **What's Missing?** Students work with a known sight word. Teacher removes one letter from the word displayed, and students identify what's missing. Builds visual memory and letter-sound awareness.
- **Mix & Fix:** Students use letter tiles/magnetic letters to build a sight word, then mix the letters and rebuild it. Students should check their work by running their finger under the word. Reinforces left-to-right sequencing and automatic word recognition.
- **Table Writing:** Students use their finger to "write" the word on the table. The finger tracing imprints the word in children's memory. Students say the word and slide their finger under it to check.
- **Whiteboards:** Students write the word on a whiteboard and say it aloud, then check with their finger. Encourages spelling the whole word, not individual letters. Do not introduce a new sight word until students have learned the current one. Limit to two days per word.

### Image 04 — Sound Boxes
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 1)**

Procedure for Elkonin-style sound boxes:
- Students say the word, then segment each sound
- Students write letters in boxes (not the sounds)
- Words should be segmented naturally
- For digraphs (sh, ch), both letters go in one box since they make one sound

**Leveled examples:**

| Level | Focus | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Level D | digraphs | sh-i-p, m-u-ch |
| Level E | initial blends | fl-a-g, sp-u-n |

Key rule: Say the word naturally, then segment each sound slowly. Students should say each sound as they write the letters in the boxes.

### Image 05 — Analogy Charts and Silent E Rule
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 1)**

**Analogy Charts by Level:**

| Level | Focus |
|-------|-------|
| Level C & D | short vowels |
| Levels C & D | Examples: -at, -op, -it |
| Levels E+ | Analogy charts begin with a known word/pattern |

**Procedures for Teaching the Silent E Rule:**
- Use a T-chart template (each student has a dry-erase marker inside)
- Put a few seconds' timer on practice
- Students write two known words (dictated by teacher) at the top
- One side: short vowel words; Other side: silent e words
- Students determine if a word has a short or long vowel and write it on the correct side

Three levels of difficulty:
1. First level: words with the same vowel sounds as key words
2. Second level (harder): same rime but vowel sound changes
3. Third level (hardest): cross words with different vowel sounds

### Image 07 — Vowel Patterns and Day 2 Overview
**Explanations of Procedures**

**Using analogy charts to teach vowel patterns:**
- Begin with known vowel patterns
- Students sort words by vowel pattern (e.g., -ow/-ow, -ore/-oar)
- Help students see how the key word connects to unknown words
- Example: If students know "road," help them read "toad"
- Combine with endings and digraphs to increase difficulty

**Day 2 Overview:**
- **Sight-Word Review, Reading, and Teaching Points** — Follow same procedures as Day 1 for sight word review, reading, and teaching points. Reserve the last ten minutes for guided writing.
- **Guided Writing (Day 2 only, 8-10 minutes):** Students write a short response to the book they have read. Writing helps students apply phonetic principles learned during word study. The writing occurs at the guided reading table and is not a seat assignment. Tremendous power is achieved when students write with teacher support.

### Image 08 — Guided Writing Options
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 2)**

Two approaches for guided writing:

**Option 1: Dictated or Student-Generated Response**
- Choose the written response for students based on their reading level
- At lower levels, dictate the sentence
- At higher levels, students generate their own response
- If students are not successful, scaffold by providing sentence starters (e.g., letter formation, a key word)

**Levels D & E: Dictated or Open-Ended Sentences**
- At these levels, dictate a few sentences for students to write
- Carefully plan the sentence to include new sight words and words that match the book
- Students should apply skills taught: blends, endings, digraphs, and vowels
- Always expect children to use what you have taught

### Image 09 — B-M-E and S-W-B-S Comprehension Strategies
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 2)**

**Levels F-I: Beginning-Middle-End (B-M-E)**
- Students write a one-sentence summary for the beginning, middle, and end of the story
- First identify something that happened at the beginning, something that happened in the middle, and how it ended
- For ELLs, have students think about the story events before they write (this is especially helpful for students learning English as a second language)

**Levels G-I: Somebody-Wanted-But-So (S-W-B-S)**
- Students write a one-sentence summary using the S-W-B-S scaffold
- At first you will need to help students write this response, but eventually they should be able to say "Write a Somebody-Wanted-But-So for this story"

| Element | Prompt |
|---------|--------|
| **Somebody** | Who is the story about? |
| **Wanted** | What did this character want? |
| **But** | But what happened? |
| **So** | So how did it end? What happened next? |

Example: "The lion wanted something to eat, but the mouse caught him, so he had to stay hungry."

### Image 10 — Teaching Points for Guided Writing
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 2)**

Critical teaching points during guided writing:

- **Rereading for meaning:** As students write, have them reread their sentences. Students should always reread to check that their writing makes sense. Only prompt for the next word if they have the rest written correctly and it makes sense.
- **Spelling sight words:** If a student misspells a sight word, use a visual prompt and say "You already know that word!" or "Try that again." If they are not successful, say "Have you seen that word in a book? Look here" and show a scaffold such as the word wall or a text.
- **Spelling unknown words:** Students should say a word slowly (not letter by letter) and write the letters they hear for each sound. Draw a box for each sound on top of the journal and have students practice writing letters in boxes before they write in their story.
- **Incorrect letter formation:** Model the correct formation on the top part of the journal. Students practice writing the letter, paying close attention to formation, size, and position.
- **Mechanics:** Expect a capital letter at the beginning and a period at the end of each sentence. Do not accept capital letters in the middle of a word.

### Image 11 — Ten-Minute Lesson for Early Readers (Individual Instruction)
**Page 136**

A structured individual intervention plan:

**Day 1: Ten Minutes**
1. **Sight-Word Review** (this is an optional review, used only if the student needs to review high-frequency words on the chart)
2. **Read a New Book** (8 minutes): Introduce the book with a brief text preview. Watch the student read. Prompt for strategies appropriate for the student's reading level. Leave the book for the student to reread on Day 2.
3. **Learn a New Sight Word** (1 minute): This activity is optional, necessary only if the student does not have the high-frequency word chart for the early guided reading lesson for levels A-E.
4. **Word Study** (3 minutes): Select one activity appropriate for the student's needs. Options include: picture sort, making words, sound boxes, or analogy chart.

**Day 2: Ten Minutes**
- **Sight-Word Review** (30 seconds): Follow same procedure as Day 1 but use different words.

### Image 12 — Day 3: Ten Minutes (Individual) and Guided Writing
**Page continuation**

**Day 3: Ten Minutes**
- **Reread** (30 seconds): Follow same procedures as Day 1. Have the student practice with a buddy in the classroom.
- **Running Record:** At some time outside of the guided reading lesson, the teacher should use the new book to take a running record for progress monitoring.

**Guided Writing (10 minutes):** Follow the procedures for guided writing:
- Since you are working with only one student, you will be able to tailor the experience
- **Levels D-E:** Dictate sentences that include known sight words and some words the student does not know how to spell. Encourage invented spelling but expect correctly taught words to be spelled correctly. Use guided reading word study focus.
- **Levels E & F: Beginning-Middle-End (B-M-E):** The student generates their own retelling of the book (one sentence about the beginning, one about the middle, and one about the ending). As the student writes, prompt them to say words slowly, add endings, and use known chunks.
- **Levels G & I:** Gradually expect students to write more during each ten-minute guided writing session. You can decide whether the student should write a B-M-E or a Somebody-Wanted-But-So (S-W-B-S). Both responses are explained in the descriptions of early guided reading lessons.

### Image 13 — Early Guided Reading Lesson: Two-Day Plan Description
**Description of Early Guided Reading Lesson (Two-Day Plan)**

**Overview:**
- **Sight word** introduced after reaching Level E
- **Time:** 1 minute per sight word
- Every child writes each sight word correctly, but some will need help
- High-frequency word chart: students should have at least six checkmarks (V) for each word
- The student should be able to write the word three times without help before considering it "known"
- Just writing it once without help does not mean the student knows the word

**Critical rule: Be sure to teach the words at the lower levels first, even if the students are reading at a higher level.**

**Introduction (Day 1 only, 3-4 minutes):**
- **Gist Statement:** Prepare a short introduction that includes the names of the characters and a brief description of the problem.
- Example: "This book is about a boy named Luke who wants in-line skates but doesn't have the money to buy them. You are going to find out how Luke and his friend Andrew find a way to earn money for the skates."

### Image 14 — Text Preview, New Vocabulary, Read and Prompt
**Explanations of Procedures (Day 1)**

**Text Preview and New Vocabulary:**
- Use the text preview to introduce new vocabulary
- Discuss key words in the context of the story (do not pre-teach every word)
- Keep previews brief and engaging
- Build necessary background knowledge through conversation, not lecture

**Read and Prompt (Days 1 & 2, First Reading, 8-10 minutes):**

> **"Do not allow choral or round-robin reading. This inhibits problem-solving behaviors and creates dependent readers."**

- It is not appropriate for students at this level to read silently
- Reading aloud (softly) will help students monitor and recall what they read
- On the second day, students have 5-8 minutes to finish reading the book
- The teacher begins the day before, if needed, to monitor the first reading
- Their fluency is the goal: they should focus on individual students, targeting their needs
- As you work with individual students, work to improve their fluency by the lesson, but also consider strategies for early readers
- Target strategy for the lesson: monitoring for meaning, decoding unfamiliar words, fluency, and comprehension

**Read the Story:** Procedures for shared/guided story reading at the early transitional level.

**Using the Thinking Cap Chart to Discuss the Story:**

### Image 16 — Early Transitional: Nonfiction Lesson (Making Maple Syrup)
**Lesson 12: Literacy Learning Objectives**

**Thinking About Nonfiction:**
- Activating prior knowledge about how nonfiction texts work differently from fiction
- Students discuss what they already know about the topic before reading
- Building schema for informational text structures

**Introduce the Nonfiction Book: Making Maple Syrup**

Numbered steps for introducing the nonfiction text:
1. "This is a book about..." (connect to student knowledge)
2. Set up a preview walk through the text features
3. Guide students to notice text features (photographs, captions, diagrams)
4. Focus vocabulary introduction on content-specific terms
5. Help students identify what kind of information they expect to learn
6. Preview pages and discuss what they see
7. Establish purpose for reading

**Key instructional focus:** How nonfiction reading strategies differ from fiction (reading for information vs. story), text features unique to informational texts, and building content vocabulary through the reading experience.

### About Dr. Jan Richardson

Dr. Jan Richardson, Ph.D. (University of Florida) is often called "America's leading expert in guided reading." She is a former Reading Recovery Teacher Leader who has taught every grade from K through college. Based in Wisconsin, she has authored seven best-selling books for Scholastic Publishing and trained thousands of teachers nationwide through her consulting practice and professional development workshops.

### Core Framework: Assess-Decide-Guide

The foundation of all Jan Richardson's work is a three-step instructional cycle:

1. **ASSESS** — Determine students' reading levels and identify specific needs through focused assessments (running records, word knowledge checks, phonics skills, fluency, and comprehension).
2. **DECIDE** — Analyze the data to form guided reading groups, select a teaching focus, and plan targeted lessons.
3. **GUIDE** — Deliver stage-specific, small-group instruction with targeted strategies, then reflect on progress.

This framework applies across all five reading stages:

| Stage | Levels | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Pre-A | Pre-readers | Letter/concept awareness |
| Emergent | A-C | Early print concepts, simple patterns |
| Early | D-I | Decoding, fluency foundations |
| Transitional | J-P | Fluency, comprehension strategies |
| Fluent | N+ | Advanced comprehension, critical thinking |

## Part 3: Synthesis — How the Photo Materials Connect to the Full Richardson System

### What the Photos Capture

The 16 photographs document the **Early Guided Reading** stage (Levels D-I), which is the critical bridge between emergent reading and transitional reading. This is where students move from pattern-based reading to actual decoding, fluency building, and comprehension strategy development.

### The Two-Day (+ Individual) Lesson Cycle

The photos reveal a highly structured lesson cycle:

**Day 1 (Group):**
1. Sight word introduction (What's Missing, Mix & Fix, Table Writing, Whiteboards)
2. Word study (Picture Sorts, Making Words, Sound Boxes, Analogy Charts — leveled D through I)
3. Text introduction (Gist Statement, vocabulary preview)
4. First reading (students read aloud softly, NO round-robin)
5. Teaching points based on observed needs

**Day 2 (Group):**
1. Sight word review
2. Continue/finish reading
3. Teaching points
4. Guided Writing (8-10 min) using B-M-E or S-W-B-S frameworks

**Day 3 (Individual — 10 minutes):**
1. Sight word review
2. New book or reread
3. Word study activity
4. Running record for progress monitoring
5. Guided writing (individually tailored)

### Critical Instructional Principles Captured

1. **No choral or round-robin reading** — Richardson explicitly forbids this, stating it "inhibits problem-solving behaviors and creates dependent readers"
2. **Leveled progression within activities** — Every word study activity (sorts, making words, sound boxes, analogy charts) has level-specific targets from D through I
3. **Integration of phonics and comprehension** — Word study and guided writing are inseparable from reading instruction
4. **Writing as a reading tool** — Guided writing is used to reinforce phonics, comprehension, and fluency simultaneously
5. **Systematic sight word mastery** — Multi-sensory approach (visual, kinesthetic, auditory) with a clear mastery criterion (6 checkmarks, 3 independent writings)
6. **ELL accommodations** — Specific modifications for English Language Learners at every stage
7. **Nonfiction integration** — Even at early transitional levels, students are introduced to nonfiction text structures and features

## Part 4: Practical Application Notes

### For a Reading Specialist Using This Material

1. **Start with assessment** — Use Richardson's assessment kits or running records to determine each student's instructional reading level (A through Z)
2. **Group by level** — Form small groups of 4-6 students reading at the same level
3. **Follow the two-day cycle** — Day 1 focuses on word study + new book introduction; Day 2 focuses on reading completion + guided writing
4. **Use the activity progressions** — The leveled activities (D through I) in these photos provide a clear roadmap for which phonics/word study activities match each reading level
5. **Monitor with the ten-minute individual lesson** — Use Day 3 for one-on-one assessment and targeted intervention
6. **Refer struggling students to RISE** — If students aren't progressing after consistent guided reading instruction, the RISE intervention provides an intensified 4-station model
