# The Science of Reading: A Complete Guide

*Compiled for the Zero to Reading series by Dr. Marie Martin, Ed.D. (USC)*

---

## What Is the Science of Reading?

The Science of Reading (SoR) is not a program you can buy. It is a vast, continuously evolving body of research from cognitive psychology, neuroscience, linguistics, and education that explains how people learn to read. It spans decades of peer-reviewed studies involving thousands of researchers across multiple disciplines.

The core finding: reading is NOT a natural process like learning to speak. Unlike oral language, which humans are biologically wired to acquire, reading must be explicitly taught. The brain has no "reading center." Instead, it repurposes regions designed for other tasks (visual processing, language comprehension, motor planning) and wires them together through instruction and practice.

## The Simple View of Reading (Gough & Tunmer, 1986)

The foundational equation:

**Reading Comprehension = Word Recognition x Language Comprehension**

These two factors are **multiplied**, not added. If either is zero, comprehension is zero. A student who can decode every word but doesn't understand oral language will not comprehend text. A student with strong vocabulary and background knowledge but who cannot decode words will also fail to comprehend.

**Word Recognition** includes:
- Phonological awareness (hearing and manipulating sounds)
- Decoding (applying letter-sound knowledge to read words)
- Sight word recognition (automatic word reading through orthographic mapping)

**Language Comprehension** includes:
- Vocabulary knowledge
- Background/world knowledge
- Understanding of sentence structure (syntax)
- Verbal reasoning and inference
- Knowledge of text structures and genres

This model makes clear why phonics-only OR comprehension-only approaches fail. Students need both strands developed simultaneously.

## Scarborough's Reading Rope (2001)

Dr. Hollis Scarborough visualized skilled reading as a rope woven from multiple strands. The metaphor is powerful: if any strand is weak, the whole rope frays.

### Upper Strands (Language Comprehension)
- **Background knowledge** (facts, concepts, world experience)
- **Vocabulary** (breadth, precision, links between words)
- **Language structures** (syntax, grammar, semantics)
- **Verbal reasoning** (inference, metaphor, critical analysis)
- **Literacy knowledge** (print concepts, genre awareness, text features)

These strands become increasingly **strategic** as readers develop.

### Lower Strands (Word Recognition)
- **Phonological awareness** (syllables, onset-rime, individual phonemes)
- **Decoding** (alphabetic principle, letter-sound patterns, phonics application)
- **Sight recognition** (automatic word reading through repeated exposure and orthographic mapping)

These strands become increasingly **automatic** as readers develop.

### Key Insight
The strands weave together into **fluent reading with comprehension**. A weakness in ANY single strand weakens the whole rope. This is why isolated phonics drill without vocabulary development produces word-callers, and why rich literature exposure without decoding instruction produces guessers.

## Ehri's Four Phases of Word Reading

Linnea Ehri's research describes how children progress through four developmental phases as they learn to read words:

### Phase 1: Pre-Alphabetic
- Children do not use letter-sound knowledge to read words
- They recognize words by visual features (the golden arches mean "McDonald's")
- They cannot decode unfamiliar words
- This is NOT reading. It is memorizing shapes.

### Phase 2: Partial Alphabetic
- Children begin using SOME letter-sound connections (usually first and last letters)
- They can identify initial sounds in words
- They often confuse similar-looking words (was/saw, bat/but)
- They are beginning to understand that letters represent sounds

### Phase 3: Full Alphabetic
- Children use complete letter-sound correspondences to decode words
- They can sound out unfamiliar words by processing each letter
- Sight word recognition develops as words are mapped into memory through repeated successful decoding
- Phonemic awareness is solid (can segment and blend 3-4 phoneme words)

### Phase 4: Consolidated Alphabetic
- Children recognize common letter patterns as units (chunks, rimes, syllables, morphemes)
- Multi-syllabic word reading becomes possible
- Sight word vocabulary expands rapidly
- Reading becomes fluent and automatic
- Cognitive resources are freed for comprehension

### Why This Matters for Instruction
- Students in Phase 1-2 need explicit, systematic phonics instruction to move forward
- Students who stay stuck in Phase 2 (using only partial cues) become habitual guessers
- The goal is to move students through Full Alphabetic to Consolidated as efficiently as possible
- Marie's automaticity cards directly target this progression: sounds (Phase 2-3), blends (Phase 3), words (Phase 3-4)

## The Five Pillars of Reading (National Reading Panel, 2000)

The National Reading Panel reviewed thousands of studies and identified five essential components of effective reading instruction:

### 1. Phonemic Awareness
The ability to hear, identify, and manipulate individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words.

- There are approximately 44 phonemes in English
- Phonemic awareness is an AUDITORY skill (no letters required)
- Key tasks: isolating sounds (/c/ in "cat"), blending sounds (/c/-/a/-/t/ = "cat"), segmenting words ("cat" = /c/-/a/-/t/), manipulating sounds (change /c/ in "cat" to /b/ = "bat")
- Most effective when taught with letters (connecting sounds to print)
- Best delivered in small groups with focused, targeted instruction

**In Marie's methodology:** Jolly Phonics actions + sound board + morning meeting review develop phonemic awareness through multi-sensory engagement. The automaticity card "Sounds" cards (Card 1) isolate and drill individual phonemes.

### 2. Phonics
Explicit, systematic instruction in the relationship between letters (graphemes) and sounds (phonemes).

- "Systematic" means sounds are taught in a planned, logical sequence
- "Explicit" means the teacher directly teaches the relationships (not hoping students figure it out from exposure)
- Synthetic phonics (teaching individual sounds then blending them into words) produces the strongest results
- Students need to apply phonics in connected reading and writing, not just worksheets

**In Marie's methodology:** Jolly Phonics provides the systematic sequence (SATIPN first, then groups 2-7). The automaticity cards provide the application (blending cards teach synthetic blending visually: s -> s-a -> s-a-t -> sat). One sound per day for Grade 3; two sounds per week for Kindergarten.

### 3. Fluency
The ability to read text accurately, at an appropriate rate, and with proper expression (prosody).

- Fluency is the bridge between decoding and comprehension
- Fluent readers free up cognitive resources to think about meaning
- Guided oral reading with feedback is highly effective
- Repeated reading of the same text builds fluency
- Students who decode laboriously cannot simultaneously comprehend

**In Marie's methodology:** The automaticity cards build word-level fluency through timed practice. The Card 3 (Words) challenge of reading scrambled words at speed without hesitation directly builds automatic word recognition. Jan Richardson's guided reading (reading aloud softly with teacher prompting) builds text-level fluency.

### 4. Vocabulary
Knowledge of word meanings, including both breadth (how many words you know) and depth (how well you know them).

- Students learn most vocabulary incidentally through reading and conversation
- Direct vocabulary instruction is also necessary, especially for academic and content-specific words
- Students need 12+ exposures to a word in meaningful contexts to truly "own" it
- Word knowledge is deeply connected to background knowledge

**In Marie's methodology:** Genki English builds spoken vocabulary for ELLs through sentence patterns and songs. Jan Richardson's text preview introduces new vocabulary before reading. Informational text first (real life topics) builds content vocabulary naturally.

### 5. Comprehension
The ability to understand, remember, and think critically about what is read. This is the ultimate goal of reading.

- Comprehension requires active engagement with text
- Students need explicit instruction in comprehension strategies
- Key strategies: monitoring understanding, making inferences, summarizing, asking questions, using text structure
- Comprehension depends on vocabulary, background knowledge, and fluency working together

**In Marie's methodology:** Comprehension is secondary until beginning reading begins (this is deliberate, not neglectful). Once students can decode, comprehension strategies are taught through Jan Richardson's framework: B-M-E (Beginning-Middle-End), S-W-B-S (Somebody-Wanted-But-So), Thinking Cap Charts, and guided writing. Informational text comes first because the content is real and accessible. Narratives follow using interactive story tools.

## How SoR Differs from Balanced Literacy and Whole Language

### Whole Language (1970s-1990s)
- Assumed reading develops naturally, like speech, through exposure to rich literature
- Minimized explicit phonics instruction
- Encouraged students to use context, pictures, and sentence structure to "guess" words
- Research consistently showed this approach left many students unable to decode

### Balanced Literacy (1990s-present)
- Attempted to add phonics to the whole language framework
- In practice, phonics was often unsystematic, brief, and disconnected from reading
- Still relied heavily on leveled readers, running records, and cueing strategies
- The "balance" often tilted toward whole language practices
- Research shows less effectiveness than structured, systematic approaches

### Science of Reading / Structured Literacy
- Grounded in decades of cognitive neuroscience and educational research
- Emphasizes explicit, systematic instruction in ALL five pillars
- Recognizes that 95% of students can learn to read with proper instruction
- Does not rely on a single program but on research-informed principles
- Effective for ALL students, and essential for students with dyslexia and reading difficulties

### The Key Difference
Whole language and balanced literacy assume children will naturally deduce how the alphabetic code works through exposure. The science of reading proves that the code must be explicitly taught, practiced to automaticity, and then applied in authentic reading and writing contexts.

## How Jan Richardson's Framework Aligns with the Science of Reading

Jan Richardson has published two white papers and a blog post addressing this directly. Her position:

### Points of Agreement
1. **SoR is research, not a program.** Many districts incorrectly search for a "Science of Reading Program" to purchase. SoR is a body of evidence that should inform instruction.

2. **Explicit, systematic phonics is essential.** All of Richardson's books include phonics scope and sequences. Her Word Study and Phonics book (with Dufresne) provides 250+ sequenced lessons.

3. **Assessment drives instruction.** Richardson's Assess-Decide-Guide framework is built on formative assessment. Small groups are formed based on data, not convenience.

4. **All five pillars must be addressed.** Richardson's lesson framework explicitly includes phonemic awareness (picture sorts, sound boxes), phonics (making words, analogy charts), fluency (rereading, guided practice), vocabulary (text previews, discussion), and comprehension (B-M-E, S-W-B-S, guided writing).

5. **Orthographic mapping matters.** Richardson teaches students to attend to ALL letters in words. Her sight word approach (What's Missing, Mix & Fix, Table Writing, Whiteboards) builds orthographic mapping through multi-sensory practice.

### Richardson's Framework Components Mapped to SoR

| Lesson Component | SoR Pillar(s) Addressed |
|---|---|
| Picture Sorts | Phonemic Awareness |
| Making Words (magnetic letters) | Phonics, Phonemic Awareness |
| Sound Boxes (Elkonin) | Phonemic Awareness, Phonics |
| Analogy Charts | Phonics (analytic + synthetic) |
| Sight Word Activities | Orthographic Mapping, Fluency |
| Text Preview & Vocabulary | Vocabulary, Background Knowledge |
| Guided Reading (read aloud softly) | Fluency, Comprehension |
| B-M-E / S-W-B-S | Comprehension |
| Guided Writing | Phonics application, Comprehension |

### Where Richardson Differs from "SoR Extremists"

Richardson agrees with SoR principles but pushes back on two extreme positions:

**1. Decodable texts as the only option:**
Richardson's position (supported by research): The evidence on decodable vs. leveled vs. multi-criteria texts is inconclusive. Some studies favor decodable texts, others do not. Her recommendation: use decodable texts during the appropriate developmental window (when students are learning to blend CVC words), but combine them with other text types. Do not use decodable texts exclusively or beyond their usefulness.

**2. The three-cueing system as "guessing":**
Richardson clarifies that the "V" in MSV (Meaning-Syntax-Visual) stands for Visual information (letter-sound relationships), NOT pictures. The three-cueing system is a diagnostic tool for analyzing student errors during running records. When a student ignores phonics, the teacher prompts for more visual information. When a student sounds out but ignores meaning, the teacher prompts for semantic checking. The goal is integration of all information sources for accurate word identification.

## How Marie's "Zero to Reading" Methodology Maps to SoR

| SoR Pillar | Marie's Approach |
|---|---|
| **Phonemic Awareness** | Jolly Phonics actions + songs, sound board, morning meeting review, name phonics ("special secrets") |
| **Phonics** | Jolly Phonics systematic sequence (SATIPN), 1 sound/day (G3) or 2 sounds/week (K), automaticity cards for practice to mastery |
| **Fluency** | Automaticity cards (timed practice), Jan Richardson guided reading (reading aloud softly), tricky words through songs |
| **Vocabulary** | Genki English sentence patterns (ELL oral language), TPR commands, Jan Richardson text previews, informational text first |
| **Comprehension** | Jan Richardson's B-M-E, S-W-B-S, Thinking Cap Charts; puzzle technique for main idea; informational text before narratives; interactive story tools for story elements |

### What Makes Marie's Approach Distinctive

1. **Speed.** Grade 3 non-readers decoded CVC words within 3-4 weeks. Kindergarteners were reading by semester 2. ELLs with zero English were communicating by December.

2. **Name phonics first.** Students' names are the most important word in the world. Starting with name sounds creates belonging and connects phonics to identity. "Special secrets" (digraphs, long vowels in names) honor the complexity of English while keeping instruction at the appropriate level.

3. **Automaticity as the gateway.** The 3-card system (Sounds -> Blends -> Words) creates measurable, self-paced mastery milestones. When students complete Book 3 cards at high speed, they can decode most CVC words. Books 4-7 add digraphs, vowel teams, and long vowels.

4. **Small group from day one.** Whole group is for singing, dancing, and practicing together. Actual instruction happens in small groups where the teacher can differentiate.

5. **ELL-integrated, not ELL-separate.** Genki English and TPR run parallel to phonics instruction, building oral language while students simultaneously learn to decode. English learners sometimes outpaced native speakers because the structured approach gave them more systematic support.

6. **Informational text first.** Real-life topics are more accessible than fictional narratives for beginning readers, especially ELLs. The language is concrete, the content connects to experience, and the text features (photos, captions) support comprehension.

7. **Comprehension is secondary UNTIL decoding is established.** This is not neglect. It is prioritization. You cannot comprehend text you cannot read. Once decoding is established, comprehension strategies are taught explicitly through Richardson's framework and Marie's interactive tools (puzzle technique, graphic organizers, KWL).
