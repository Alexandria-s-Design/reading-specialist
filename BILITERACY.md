# Biliteracy & ELL Best Practices: Research Frameworks for Zero to Reading

*Compiled by Dr. Marie Martin, Ed.D. (USC)*
*Part of the Zero to Reading Series*

---

## Why Biliteracy Matters

California has over 1.1 million English Language Learners in public schools. Superintendent Tony Thurmond's mandate to get kids reading cannot be achieved without addressing how bilingual and multilingual children learn to read. The research is clear: bilingualism is an asset, not a deficit. When instruction builds on students' home language, reading outcomes improve in BOTH languages.

This document synthesizes the top biliteracy researchers and their frameworks, maps them to the Zero to Reading methodology, and provides practical guidance for K-3 teachers working with ELLs.

## Part 1: The Foundational Theories Every Teacher Must Know

### 1. Jim Cummins — BICS/CALP and Common Underlying Proficiency

**Institution:** University of Toronto, Ontario Institute for Studies in Education

**The Two Proficiencies:**
- **BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communication Skills):** Conversational fluency. Acquired in 1-3 years. Students sound fluent on the playground. Teachers may assume the student "speaks English" and no longer needs support.
- **CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency):** Academic language. Takes 5-7+ years to develop. The language of textbooks, content instruction, standardized tests, and academic discussion. A student who can chat with friends may struggle with a science text.

**Why this matters for K-3:** A first-grader who arrived in Kindergarten with zero English may sound conversationally fluent by second grade. Teachers often remove ELL services too early because the child "sounds fine." But academic reading comprehension, writing in content areas, and complex text analysis require CALP, which is still years away. Keep supporting these students.

**Common Underlying Proficiency (CUP) / The Iceberg Model:**
Cummins' most powerful idea: literacy skills, conceptual knowledge, and learning strategies transfer across languages. The surface features of each language differ (vocabulary, grammar, pronunciation), but underneath, there is a shared cognitive foundation.

- A student who learns to decode in Spanish transfers that decoding ability to English
- A student who learns story structure in Arabic can apply that knowledge to English narratives
- A student who learns to summarize in Mandarin can summarize in English once they have the vocabulary
- Conceptual knowledge (what a volcano is, how fractions work) transfers regardless of which language it was learned in

**The Iceberg Metaphor:** Two languages are like two icebergs. Above the waterline, they look separate. Below the waterline, they share the same base. Instruction in one language enriches the whole system.

**Threshold Hypothesis:** Students need to reach a minimum level of proficiency in both languages to experience the cognitive benefits of bilingualism. Below that threshold, a student may feel caught between two languages without solid footing in either. This is why "subtractive bilingualism" (replacing L1 with L2) produces worse outcomes than "additive bilingualism" (adding L2 while maintaining L1).

**Cummins' Quadrant Model:** Classifies tasks along two dimensions:
- Context-embedded (visuals, gestures, real objects) vs. context-reduced (text-only, abstract)
- Cognitively undemanding (routine, familiar) vs. cognitively demanding (new concepts, complex thinking)

For K-3 ELLs, start in the context-embedded/cognitively undemanding quadrant (conversations with pictures, hands-on activities) and gradually move toward context-reduced/cognitively demanding tasks (reading comprehension, academic writing).

**Key Publications:**
- *Negotiating Identities: Education for Empowerment in a Diverse Society* (2001)
- *Language, Power, and Pedagogy: Bilingual Children in the Crossfire* (2000)
- *Bilingualism and Special Education: Issues in Assessment and Pedagogy* (1984)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie's use of Genki English builds BICS through structured sentence patterns. Her Jolly Phonics approach builds decoding skills that transfer across languages (CUP). Her "special secrets" name phonics approach affirms identity, exactly as Cummins recommends. Her ELL students often outpaced native speakers because the explicit, systematic approach gave them stronger foundations than the implicit exposure many native speakers relied on.

### 2. Stephen Krashen — Comprehensible Input and the Affective Filter

**Institution:** University of Southern California

**Five Hypotheses of Second Language Acquisition:**

1. **The Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis:** "Acquisition" (subconscious, through meaningful communication) is more powerful than "learning" (conscious study of grammar rules). Children acquire language naturally when they receive meaningful input.

2. **The Monitor Hypothesis:** Conscious grammar knowledge acts as an editor, not a generator. Students produce language through acquisition and then check it with learned rules. Over-monitoring causes hesitancy and anxiety.

3. **The Input Hypothesis (i+1):** Language is acquired when learners receive input that is slightly above their current level. "i" is current competence; "i+1" is the next step. The key is COMPREHENSIBLE input, not just any input.

4. **The Affective Filter Hypothesis:** Anxiety, low motivation, and poor self-confidence raise an emotional barrier that blocks language acquisition. When students feel safe, valued, and motivated, the filter drops and acquisition accelerates.

5. **The Natural Order Hypothesis:** Grammatical structures are acquired in a predictable order. Explicit grammar instruction cannot change this order. Focus on meaningful communication, not grammar sequencing.

**Free Voluntary Reading:** Krashen's research shows that free voluntary reading (reading for pleasure, by choice) is the single most powerful tool for developing vocabulary, grammar, writing ability, and reading comprehension in both L1 and L2.

**Key Publications:**
- *The Input Hypothesis: Issues and Implications* (1985)
- *The Power of Reading* (2004)
- *Principles and Practice in Second Language Acquisition* (1982)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Everything Marie does aligns with Krashen. Genki English provides comprehensible input at i+1 through structured sentence patterns with game-based practice. TPR lowers the affective filter (students participate physically without pressure to speak). Songs and movement create low-anxiety environments. Her classroom was joyful: "We had SO much fun." That fun is not just nice to have. It is scientifically critical. When students are anxious, language acquisition slows. When they feel safe and excited, it accelerates.

### 3. Ofelia Garcia — Translanguaging

**Institution:** City University of New York (CUNY)

**What is Translanguaging?**
Translanguaging challenges the idea that bilinguals have two separate language systems. Instead, bilingual students have ONE integrated linguistic repertoire. They draw on their full range of linguistic resources to make meaning, learn, and communicate.

Translanguaging is NOT:
- Code-switching (which implies separate systems being alternated)
- "Lazy mixing" of languages
- A sign that students don't know either language well

Translanguaging IS:
- Using all linguistic resources to think, learn, and communicate
- A sophisticated cognitive practice that reflects bilingual competence
- A pedagogical strategy that legitimizes students' full identity
- A way to deepen comprehension by processing in multiple languages

**In the K-3 classroom, translanguaging looks like:**
- A student reading in English and discussing the text in Spanish with a partner to deepen understanding, then writing a response in English
- A student using Spanish phonics knowledge to decode an unfamiliar English word ("that looks like 'familia'... family!")
- A student writing a story that includes both languages because that reflects how they think and live
- A teacher saying "tell me about this in whatever language helps you think" during comprehension discussions

**Key Publications:**
- *Bilingual Education in the 21st Century: A Global Perspective* (2009)
- *Translanguaging: Language, Bilingualism, and Education* (with Li Wei, 2014)
- *Educating Emergent Bilinguals* (with Jo Anne Kleifgen, 2018)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie naturally used translanguaging in Abu Dhabi. Students processed phonics instruction in English but discussed with peers in Arabic. The "special secrets" in names (like the /th/ in Beth) acknowledge that English has its own patterns that may differ from students' home language, but rather than framing this as confusion, Marie frames it as discovery. The key insight: don't punish students for using their home language in the classroom. It is their most powerful learning tool.

### 4. Nancy Hornberger — Continua of Biliteracy

**Institution:** University of Pennsylvania

**The Continua Model:**
Hornberger's framework describes biliteracy development not as stages or levels but as intersecting continua (spectrums). Literacy develops along multiple dimensions simultaneously:

- **Context:** Micro (classroom) to macro (policy/society)
- **Development:** Oral to written; receptive (listening/reading) to productive (speaking/writing)
- **Content:** Minority language to majority language; vernacular to literary; contextualized to decontextualized
- **Media:** Simultaneous biliteracy to sequential; similar scripts to dissimilar; convergent languages to divergent

**The Power of the Continua Model:** Traditional approaches privilege the "powerful" end of each continuum (majority language, written, decontextualized, standard/literary). Hornberger argues that educators should attend to the traditionally marginalized ends (minority language, oral, contextualized, vernacular) because these are the foundations biliteracy is built on.

**Key Publications:**
- *Continua of Biliteracy: An Ecological Framework for Educational Policy, Research, and Practice in Multilingual Settings* (2003)
- Numerous journal articles in *TESOL Quarterly*, *Language Policy*, *International Journal of Bilingualism*

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie starts with oral language (TPR, Genki English, songs) before written literacy. She starts with contextualized, concrete content (informational text about real life) before abstract narrative. She values students' home language as a resource. She starts with the student's name (the most personal, identity-rich language possible). All of this aligns with Hornberger's principle of building from the traditionally marginalized ends of the continua toward the traditionally privileged ends.

## Part 2: K-3 Biliteracy Implementation Frameworks

### 5. Kathy Escamilla — Literacy Squared (Liter2)

**Institution:** University of Colorado Boulder

**The Most K-3 Specific Biliteracy Framework**

Literacy Squared rejects the sequential model (learn to read in L1 first, then transfer to L2). Instead, it advocates for **simultaneous biliteracy development** from the very beginning.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Teach literacy in both languages from Kindergarten.** Don't wait for one language to be "ready" before starting the other.
2. **Use "The Bridge"** as a formal instructional practice. After teaching a reading or writing lesson, explicitly guide students to make cross-language connections: "In Spanish we wrote /casa/. In English, the word is /house/. What do you notice about how the sounds are different?"
3. **Do NOT measure bilingual students against monolingual norms.** A bilingual first-grader's writing in English will look different from a monolingual first-grader's writing. This is not a deficit. It is a stage in biliteracy development.
4. **Bilingual pairs:** Pair bilingual students for literacy activities so they can use both languages to process, discuss, and extend their learning.
5. **Holistic assessment:** Evaluate what a student can do across BOTH languages. A child who writes 3 sentences in English and 5 in Spanish has a total literacy output of 8 sentences, not a deficit of 2 sentences.

**"The Bridge" in Practice (K-3):**
After a reading lesson in English, the teacher pauses and says:
- "We just read about [topic] in English. How would you say [key word] in Spanish/Arabic/Vietnamese?"
- "The word 'rain' starts with /r/. In Spanish, the word is 'lluvia.' Does it start with the same sound? No, it starts with /y/. But both words mean the same thing."
- "We used the word 'because' in our writing today. In Spanish, that word is 'porque.' Where have you seen 'porque' in your reading?"

The Bridge makes cross-language transfer EXPLICIT rather than hoping students figure it out on their own.

**Key Publications:**
- *Biliteracy from the Start: Literacy Squared in Action* (with Susan Hopewell & others, 2014)
- Numerous articles in *Bilingual Research Journal* and *Journal of Literacy Research*

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie's "special secrets" in names IS a Bridge activity. When she tells a student "T and H are best friends and make the /th/ sound," she is making explicit a cross-linguistic difference that the student would otherwise have to discover alone. Her use of Jolly Phonics (English phonics) alongside students' home language is simultaneous biliteracy in practice. The automaticity cards could be extended to include partner-language versions for dual-language classrooms.

### 6. SIOP — Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol

**Developers:** Jana Echevarria (CSU Long Beach), MaryEllen Vogt (CSU Long Beach), Deborah Short (Center for Applied Linguistics)

**The most widely used ELL instructional model in US classrooms.** Validated through a 7-year CREDE research project.

**The 8 Components:**

| Component | What It Means | K-3 Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Lesson Preparation** | Every lesson has both a content objective AND a language objective | Content: "Students will identify CVC words." Language: "Students will say each sound smoothly when blending." |
| **2. Building Background** | Link new content to students' prior knowledge, cultural experiences, and past learning | Before a lesson on weather, ask: "What is the weather like where your family is from? How do you say 'rain' in your language?" |
| **3. Comprehensible Input** | Use clear speech, visuals, gestures, demonstrations, realia (real objects) | Jolly Phonics actions, sound board display, picture cards alongside word cards |
| **4. Strategies** | Teach cognitive and metacognitive strategies explicitly; scaffold thinking | "When I don't know a word, I look at the first sound, then blend..." (think-aloud) |
| **5. Interaction** | Frequent structured talk opportunities; varied grouping; wait time | Partner blending practice, small group guided reading, turn-and-talk |
| **6. Practice/Application** | Hands-on activities integrating language practice with content | Automaticity card centers, magnetic letter word building, guided writing |
| **7. Lesson Delivery** | Objectives supported; students engaged; appropriate pacing | Quick-paced sound drill, then slowed-down blending instruction, then timed practice |
| **8. Review/Assessment** | Regular formative assessment; key vocabulary reviewed | Running records, automaticity card timing records, morning meeting sound review |

**Key Publication:**
- *Making Content Comprehensible for English Learners: The SIOP Model* (multiple editions)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie's approach is SIOP in practice without using the label. Every element maps:
- Lesson preparation: Marie's daily sequence has clear phonics targets (content) + specific sounds/blends to produce (language)
- Building background: Name phonics connects to students' identity and home language
- Comprehensible input: Jolly Phonics actions, sound board, songs, TPR are all multi-sensory comprehensible input
- Interaction: Small group rotation, partner practice, Genki English games
- Practice/Application: Automaticity card centers, guided writing
- Review/Assessment: Morning meeting sound review, running records, card timing

### 7. GLAD — Guided Language Acquisition Design

**Developer:** Marcia Brechtel; now expanded as E.L. Achieve

**Heavily used in California K-3 classrooms.** A multi-sensory, high-engagement model that integrates language development into content instruction.

**Key Strategies and Their K-3 Reading Applications:**

| GLAD Strategy | What It Is | Reading Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Pictorial Input Chart** | Teacher draws and narrates a content-rich picture while students observe | Before reading an informational text, draw the key concepts while narrating. Students see AND hear vocabulary. |
| **Cognitive Content Dictionary** | Collaborative vocabulary building: word, prediction, definition, illustration, sentence | Before guided reading, build a class dictionary of key words from the text |
| **Sentence Patterning Charts** | Interactive charts where students generate sentences following patterns | "The [animal] can [verb]. The [animal] has [noun]." Students create sentences using reading vocabulary. |
| **10/2 Lecture** | 10 min of input, 2 min of student processing (turn-and-talk, sketch) | After 10 minutes of shared reading, students turn to a partner and retell what happened |
| **Chants and Songs** | Rhythmic, repetitive language for internalizing vocabulary and structures | Jolly Phonics songs, Genki English sentence chants, tricky word songs |
| **Process Grid** | Chart organizing information across categories | After reading, students sort story elements (who, what, where, when) into a grid |

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie uses GLAD strategies naturally. Her Genki English sentence patterns ARE sentence patterning charts delivered through song. Her sound board IS a pictorial input display. Her tricky word songs ARE the chants/songs strategy. Her morning meeting review IS the 10/2 principle (brief input followed by student practice). Her small group rotation IS the cooperative learning structure GLAD requires.

### 8. Virginia Collier & Wayne Thomas — The Prism Model

**Institution:** George Mason University

**The Prism Model describes four interdependent dimensions of language acquisition:**

1. **Sociocultural:** The social and cultural context of learning. Does the student feel welcome? Is their culture valued? Are their family and community respected?
2. **Linguistic:** Language development in L1 AND L2. Oral language, reading, writing in both languages.
3. **Academic:** Grade-level content knowledge in all subjects. ELLs must access the same rigorous content as all students.
4. **Cognitive:** Thinking skills, problem-solving, creativity. Must be developed through the home language while L2 is being acquired, or there is a cognitive gap.

**The critical finding from 30+ years of longitudinal data:**
Dual language education programs are the ONLY instructional model that fully closes the achievement gap between ELLs and native English speakers. English-only approaches, ESL pullout, and transitional bilingual programs all leave a gap. Only two-way immersion and developmental bilingual programs produce full academic parity by grade 5-6.

**Key Publications:**
- *Educating English Learners for a Transformed World* (2017)
- *Dual Language Education for a Transformed World* (2012)
- Multiple research reports from the Center for Research on Education, Diversity & Excellence (CREDE)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie addresses all four Prism dimensions:
- Sociocultural: Name phonics creates belonging. Genki English songs celebrate communication. TPR makes the classroom joyful and inclusive.
- Linguistic: Jolly Phonics (L2 decoding) + oral home language support + Genki English (L2 speaking)
- Academic: Informational text first (real content, not watered down). Jan Richardson's guided reading at grade-appropriate levels.
- Cognitive: Small group instruction maintains cognitive challenge. Students are not placed in "low groups" indefinitely.

### 9. WIDA Framework

**Used by 41 states and territories for ELL identification, instruction, and assessment.**

**Six Proficiency Levels:**

| Level | Name | What Students Can Do (K-3 Reading) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Entering | Match words to pictures. Identify letters. Follow along with shared reading using illustrations. |
| 2 | Emerging | Read simple sentences with picture support. Identify main characters. Sequence 2-3 events with visual cues. |
| 3 | Developing | Read grade-level text with some support. Identify main idea. Answer comprehension questions in short phrases. |
| 4 | Expanding | Read and comprehend grade-level text with minimal support. Summarize. Make inferences with text evidence. |
| 5 | Bridging | Read at or near grade level. Write detailed responses. Engage in literary analysis. |
| 6 | Reaching | Full proficiency. Performs comparably to English-proficient peers. |

**Can Do Descriptors:** WIDA provides asset-based statements about what students CAN do at each level, rather than focusing on what they cannot do. This is critical for K-3 teachers: instead of "This student can't read grade-level text," the framing is "This student can match key vocabulary to pictures and follow a story using illustrations. Next steps: build sight word vocabulary and introduce decodable text with picture support."

**Key Document:**
- *WIDA English Language Development Standards Framework, 2020 Edition*

**Connection to Marie's methodology:** Marie's differentiated pacing IS WIDA-informed instruction:
- Level 1-2 students: TPR, Genki English basic sentences, Jolly Phonics sounds with heavy visual support, automaticity Card 1 (sounds only)
- Level 3-4 students: Blending words, tricky word songs, beginning guided reading with informational text, automaticity Cards 2-3
- Level 5-6 students: Full guided reading cycle, comprehension strategies, long vowel cards, writing

### 10. California ELD Framework

**The most detailed state framework for ELL instruction in the nation.** Three-part structure:

1. **Integrated ELD:** Language development embedded within content teaching. ALL teachers do this for ALL ELLs, in every subject. During a reading lesson, the teacher explicitly teaches both the reading skill AND the language needed to demonstrate understanding.

2. **Designated ELD:** Protected time for explicit English language instruction at students' proficiency level. This is IN ADDITION to content instruction, not instead of it. Students are grouped by English proficiency (not reading level) for focused language work.

3. **ELD in Support of Foundational Skills:** Specific guidance for connecting language development to phonemic awareness, phonics, and fluency. Addresses: "How do you teach phonics to a student whose home language has different sounds than English?"

**Proficiency Levels:** Emerging, Expanding, Bridging (simpler than WIDA's six levels)

**Connection to Marie's methodology:**
- Marie's Jolly Phonics small groups = Integrated ELD (phonics content + language practice together)
- Marie's Genki English sessions = Designated ELD (focused oral language development)
- Marie's approach to sounds not present in students' home languages (teaching regular sounds first, then revealing "special secrets") = ELD in Support of Foundational Skills

### ELL Best Practices Embedded in Every Lesson

These practices should be present in EVERY reading lesson, not just "ELL lessons":

**Before Reading:**
- Activate prior knowledge in ANY language ("Tell your partner what you know about [topic] in whatever language you think in")
- Front-load vocabulary with visuals, gestures, and real objects (SIOP Component 2 + 3)
- Preview-View-Review: briefly preview key ideas in students' home language if possible (Freeman & Freeman)
- Set both a content objective AND a language objective (SIOP Component 1)

**During Reading:**
- Allow translanguaging: students can discuss with partners in home language to process meaning (Garcia)
- Provide sentence frames for responses: "The main idea is ___. One detail is ___." (GLAD sentence patterning)
- Use gestures, sound effects, and dramatic reading to make text comprehensible (Krashen's i+1)
- Pause every 2-3 pages for partner processing (GLAD's 10/2 principle)

**After Reading:**
- Bridge explicitly: "We read this word in English. How would you say it in your language?" (Escamilla)
- Use graphic organizers that reduce language demand while maintaining cognitive demand (Cummins' Quadrant)
- Allow responses in drawings, L1, or a mix of languages before requiring full English responses (Hornberger's continua)
- Celebrate what students CAN do, not what they cannot (WIDA's Can Do philosophy)

**Classroom Environment:**
- Sound board displays all learned sounds (multi-sensory reference)
- Word wall includes cognates when applicable ("family / familia")
- Student names displayed with their "special secrets" (identity affirmation, Cummins)
- Morning meeting includes TPR, songs in multiple languages, sound review
- Small groups are the default. Whole group is for community building (songs, dance, shared experiences)
- The affective filter stays LOW: fun, movement, no punishment for mistakes, celebration of approximation

### Protecting the Home Language (Wong Fillmore's Warning)

Lily Wong Fillmore's research at UC Berkeley found that children can lose their home language within 2-3 years of entering English-only schooling. This language loss:
- Damages communication with parents and grandparents
- Erodes cultural identity and family bonds
- Eliminates the cognitive advantages of bilingualism
- Removes the cross-linguistic transfer foundation that Cummins identified

**What K-3 Teachers Should Do:**
1. NEVER tell parents to "speak only English at home." This is the single most damaging piece of advice a teacher can give. Research consistently shows that strong L1 development supports L2 acquisition.
2. Encourage parents to read to children in their home language. Reading in ANY language builds literacy foundations.
3. Include bilingual books in the classroom library.
4. Allow students to use their home language during peer discussions and writing brainstorming.
5. Celebrate multilingualism publicly: "How many languages do we speak in our class? That's amazing!"
6. Send home materials in families' home languages when possible.

### The Seal of Biliteracy: The Long Game

49+ states now offer the Seal of Biliteracy, a recognition on the high school diploma for students who demonstrate proficiency in two or more languages. This creates a K-12 pathway:

- **Kindergarten:** Students enter with home language assets. Instruction builds on both languages.
- **Grades 1-3:** Simultaneous biliteracy development. Students read and write in both languages.
- **Grades 4-8:** Academic language deepens in both languages. Content-area literacy in L1 and L2.
- **Grades 9-12:** Students demonstrate bilingual proficiency and earn the Seal.

**For K-3 teachers:** Frame your biliteracy instruction not as remediation but as the first step on a path to a state-recognized credential. Tell parents: "Your child's bilingualism is not a problem to be solved. It is an advantage we are building on. By graduation, your child could earn the Seal of Biliteracy."
