*By Dr. Marie Martin, Ed.D. (USC)*
*Field-tested in Abu Dhabi (UAE) and California (USA)*

## The Promise

Grade 3 students who cannot read can decode CVC words within 3-4 weeks. Kindergarteners who enter school with no letter knowledge are reading by semester 2. English Language Learners with zero English are communicating and reading alongside their peers by winter break.

This is not theory. This happened in real classrooms in Abu Dhabi and in US public schools. This framework documents exactly how.

## Phase 1: Sound Foundation

### Pacing

| Grade Level | Pacing | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 3 (intervention) | 1 sound per day | 2-3 weeks for all 42 sounds |
| Kindergarten | 2 sounds per week | ~10 weeks for Groups 1-3 |
| Kindergarten (advanced) | Can accelerate once short vowels are solid | Teacher judgment |

### Day 1 / Week 1: Name Phonics

> **Biliteracy Connection (Cummins):** Starting with students' names affirms identity and validates their full linguistic self. Cummins' research shows that identity affirmation is a prerequisite for language learning. A student whose name and language are celebrated will learn faster than one whose home language is ignored.

Before touching a single Jolly Phonics sound, students learn the sounds in their own names. Their name is the most important word in the world.

**Procedure:**
1. Write each student's name on a card
2. Point to each letter and teach its sound (the regular, level-1 sound)
3. When a letter combination makes a different sound than expected, tell the student: "Your name holds a special secret"
4. Teach the "regular" sound first, then reveal the secret

**Examples:**
- **Beth**: Teach /b/, /e/, /t/, /h/ as individual sounds. Then reveal: "T and H are best friends. When they come together, they make the /th/ sound. That's your name's special secret."
- **Jackson**: Students learn /j/, /a/, /c/, /k/, /s/, /o/, /n/. Then reveal: "C and K are best friends in your name."
- **Kate**: Student learns /k/, /a/, /t/, /e/. The "special secret" is that the E at the end makes the A say its own name (long vowel). But at this stage, you still have the student say each sound in its regular form during sound-out activities.

**Why this works:**
- Creates immediate belonging and investment
- Connects abstract phonics to personal identity
- Introduces phonics concepts (digraphs, silent e, long vowels) as exciting discoveries, not confusing rules
- Students remember their name's "secret" and become curious about other secrets in English
- For ELLs: the name bridge connects home language sounds to English sounds (Escamilla's "Bridge" technique). Example: "Your name is /Muhammad/. In Arabic, that /m/ sound is the same as in English! Your languages share that sound."

### Jolly Phonics Sound Sequence

Sounds are taught in the Jolly Phonics order, designed so students can begin blending words as early as Group 1:

| Group | Sounds | Why This Order |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | s, a, t, i, p, n | High frequency. Enables CVC words immediately: sat, pin, tan, tip, nap, sit, pat, nip, tap |
| **2** | c, k, e, h, r, m, d | Adds more consonants + short /e/. Words: cat, hen, red, men, kit, cap, kid, map, dim |
| **3** | g, o, u, l, f, b | Completes short vowels. Words: got, bug, log, fun, big, leg, hut, fog, lip, bed |
| **4** | ai, j, oa, ie, ee, or | First vowel digraphs. Words: rain, coat, tree, born, jail |
| **5** | z, w, ng, v, oo | More sounds. Words: zip, look, moon, wing, van |
| **6** | y, x, ch, sh, th | Consonant digraphs. Words: dish, this, chop, yes, wax |
| **7** | qu, ou, oi, ue, er, ar | Remaining patterns. Words: foil, hard, quack, blue, shout |

### Multi-Sensory Teaching for Each Sound

Every sound is taught with THREE sensory channels (Jolly Phonics method):

1. **Visual**: Show the letter(s). Write them. Display on the sound board.
2. **Auditory**: Say the sound. Sing the Jolly Phonics song for that sound. Repeat.
3. **Kinesthetic**: Do the Jolly Phonics action/movement for that sound. Students practice the movement every time they say the sound.

### Sound Board

A classroom display where every learned sound is posted and visible. Updated daily/weekly as new sounds are taught.

**Morning Meeting Review Routine:**
- Every morning during morning meeting, review ALL sounds on the board
- Point to each sound. Students say the sound and do the movement together.
- This takes 2-3 minutes and ensures continuous review
- By the time all 42 sounds are taught, students have had dozens of exposures to the early sounds

### Begin Blending IMMEDIATELY

> **Biliteracy Connection (Krashen):** Blending immediately provides comprehensible input at i+1. Students already know the individual sounds (i). Combining them into words is the next step (+1). The affective filter stays low because students are working with sounds they already mastered on the automaticity cards.

Do not wait until all sounds are taught to start blending. After Group 1 (SATIPN, 6 sounds), students begin blending CVC words the same day or the next day.

**Smooth Blending Technique:**
- Students say each sound continuously, stretching continuous sounds (sssss-aaaa-ttttt)
- No choppy, disconnected sounds. The sounds flow into each other.
- Teacher models: "Watch my mouth. Sssssaaaattt. Sat."
- Students practice with CVC words using only the sounds they know

## Phase 2: Blending and CVC Mastery

### The Automaticity Card System

> **Biliteracy Connection (Ehri + Cummins):** The automaticity cards move students through Ehri's phases of word reading (partial alphabetic to full alphabetic to consolidated). Because decoding is a transferable skill (Cummins' CUP), students who build automaticity in English phonics can transfer that decoding ability to their home language and vice versa. A Spanish-speaking student who masters blending in English can apply the same blending strategy to Spanish words.

Marie's original creation (By Alexandria's World). A 3-card timed progression system aligned to each Jolly Phonics book.

**Setup:** Cut each page in half, mount on card stock, laminate. Place at a center station with a timer.

#### Card 1 — SOUNDS
A 5x5 grid of individual letter sounds in scrambled order.
- Read left to right, 5 per row
- Numbers on right side (5, 10, 15, 20, 25) track how many sounds read
- Students say each sound without hesitation
- **Rule: If you hesitate, start over**
- Goal: read all 25 sounds fluently at speed

**Example (Book 1):**

| | | | | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| s | a | t | i | p | 5 |
| a | t | s | n | i | 10 |
| p | n | a | i | t | 15 |
| t | s | i | p | n | 20 |
| n | p | t | s | i | 25 |

#### Card 2 — BLENDS
A 4-row waterfall showing progressive word building. Read top to bottom per column.
- Row 1: Single consonant (s)
- Row 2: Consonant + vowel (s-a)
- Row 3: Add final sound (s-a-t)
- Row 4: Full word (sat)
- 5 words per card

**Example (Book 1):**

| | | | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| s | t | n | p | t |
| s-a | t-i | n-a | p-i | t-i |
| s-a-t | t-i-n | n-a-p | p-i-t | t-i-p |
| sat | tin | nap | pit | tip |

#### Card 3 — WORDS
The same 5 words from Card 2, scrambled in a 5x5 grid (different order per row).
- Read left to right at speed
- No hesitation allowed
- **The fun is going FASTER** while maintaining accuracy
- When students complete Card 3 for a book at high speed, they have mastered that book's words

**Example (Book 1):**

| | | | | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| tan | pit | sip | tap | pan | 5 |
| pit | sip | tan | pan | tap | 10 |
| sip | tan | pan | pit | tap | 15 |
| tap | pan | pit | sip | tan | 20 |
| pan | tap | sip | pit | tan | 25 |

### Progression Through Books

| After completing... | Students can... |
|---|---|
| Book 1 cards (SATIPN) | Read CVC words with s, a, t, i, p, n |
| Book 2 cards (CKREMDH) | Read CVC words with most common consonants |
| **Book 3 cards (GOULFB)** | **Read virtually ALL CVC words** |
| Book 4 cards | Read words with vowel digraphs (ai, oa, ie, ee, or) |
| Book 5 cards | Read words with z, w, ng, v, oo |
| Book 6 cards | Read words with ch, sh, th, y, x |
| Book 7 cards | Read words with qu, ou, oi, ue, er, ar |
| Long Vowel cards | Read words with all long vowel patterns (a-e, ai, ay, ee, ea, ie, igh, oa, ow, ue, ew) |

**Marie tells students: "When you finish Book 3 cards at a high level, you can pretty much read. Then we get to have fun learning the rules."** Books 4-7 are the "fun rules" phase.

### Color-Coded Variants

Each book's cards come in 6 color variants (pink/red, orange, yellow, green, teal, purple). This allows:
- Multiple students to work at different stations simultaneously
- Visual tracking of which set a student is working on
- Center rotation without confusion

### Tricky Words (Sight Words via Songs)

Marie does NOT teach sight words through flash card memorization. Instead:
- "Tricky words" are words that don't follow the regular phonics rules students have learned
- Taught through songs, chants, and rhythmic repetition
- Practiced in context (in readers and in writing)
- Word arrangements: students build sentences using known tricky words (I can, I want, You will, I like)

**With her own daughter:** She learned "I like" and then did word arrangements: "I like cats. I like to run. I like the park." The tricky words became automatic through USE, not memorization.

## Phase 3: Transition to Guided Reading

### When to Transition

Students are ready for Jan Richardson's guided reading framework when they can:
- Decode CVC words with reasonable accuracy and speed (Book 3 automaticity cards at a solid level)
- Recognize some tricky words automatically
- Demonstrate basic blending without excessive hesitation

### Small Group Formation

**NEVER whole group instruction for reading.** Whole group is for:
- Singing together (Jolly Phonics songs, Genki English songs, tricky word songs)
- Dancing and movement (TPR, Jolly Phonics actions)
- Morning meeting sound board review
- Shared experiences

Actual reading instruction happens in **small groups** formed by assessment:
- Use Jan Richardson's assessment framework (or running records) to determine each student's reading level
- Group students reading at similar levels (4-6 per group)
- Groups are FLEXIBLE. Reassess regularly and move students as they progress.

### Informational Text First

> **Biliteracy Connection (Goldenberg + Cummins' Quadrant):** Informational text is context-embedded and cognitively accessible, placing it in the ideal quadrant for ELLs. The content connects to real-world knowledge that students already have in their home language. A student who knows about rain in Arabic can leverage that background knowledge when reading about weather in English. This is Cummins' CUP in action: conceptual knowledge transfers.

Marie starts guided reading with **informational/nonfiction text** before narratives. Reasons:
- The content is real life and connects to students' experiences
- The language is concrete and direct
- Text features (photographs, captions, labels, diagrams) support comprehension
- Students find real-world content inherently interesting
- For ELLs, concrete topics are more accessible than abstract narratives

### Jan Richardson's Day 1-2 Cycle

Once students are reading leveled texts, follow Richardson's Early Guided Reading framework:

**Day 1:**
1. Sight word introduction (What's Missing, Mix & Fix, Table Writing, Whiteboards)
2. Word study activity matched to level (picture sorts, making words, sound boxes, analogy charts)
3. Text introduction (gist statement, vocabulary preview)
4. First reading (students read aloud softly, NO round-robin, NO choral reading)
5. Teaching points based on observed needs

**Day 2:**
1. Sight word review
2. Continue/finish reading
3. Teaching points
4. Guided writing (8-10 minutes) using B-M-E or S-W-B-S frameworks

**Day 3 (Individual, 10 minutes):**
1. Sight word review
2. New book or reread
3. Word study activity
4. Running record for progress monitoring

### Narrative Text Introduction

After students have experience with informational text, introduce narratives using:
- Cross-cultural fairy tales and folktales (Cinderella variants from different cultures)
- Interactive story element tools (setting, characters, conflict, resolution)
- The learner.org "Elements of a Story" approach (now rebuilt as our own interactive tool)

## Phase 4: Long Vowels and Advanced Decoding

### Differentiated Long Vowel Instruction

**For students who arrive at Kindergarten already knowing their short vowels:**
- Move through short vowel review in the first 5 weeks
- Begin long vowel instruction immediately in small groups
- Use the Long Vowel automaticity cards (Sounds, Blends, Words for Long A, E, I, O, U)

**For students on the standard pacing:**
- Long vowels are introduced explicitly in the last semester
- Taught in small groups, not whole group
- Each long vowel is taught for several weeks
- Multiple spelling patterns per vowel (e.g., Long A: ai, ay, a-e)

### The Automaticity Cards for Long Vowels

The Books 4-7 and Long Vowel cards extend the same 3-card system:
- **Sounds cards** now include digraphs and long vowel patterns (ai, oa, ie, ee, or, etc.)
- **Blend cards** show the waterfall with new patterns: r -> r-ai -> r-ai-n -> rain
- **Word cards** test speed reading of words with long vowel patterns: play, mail, tray, rate, base
- Red highlighting on blend cards shows where special rules apply (e.g., a-e in "rate" shown in red)

## ELL Track (Parallel Throughout)

> **Biliteracy Frameworks Active in This Track:**
> - **Krashen:** Comprehensible input (i+1) through graded sentence patterns. Low affective filter through songs and games.
> - **Cummins:** Building BICS through structured oral language. Additive bilingualism (L2 added, L1 maintained).
> - **Garcia:** Translanguaging allowed and encouraged. Students process in L1 while producing in L2.
> - **SIOP:** Every activity has both a content objective and a language objective.
> - **GLAD:** Chants, songs, sentence patterning, pictorial input, cooperative structures.
> - **Collier & Thomas:** All four Prism dimensions addressed (sociocultural, linguistic, academic, cognitive).
> - **Wong Fillmore:** Home language protected. Parents encouraged to maintain L1 at home.
> - **Escamilla:** "The Bridge" used to make cross-language connections explicit.

### Genki English for Oral Language

Marie used Genki English to teach ELLs to speak in common phrases. The pacing progressed as students gained confidence:
- **First weeks:** One new sentence pattern every 2-3 weeks
- **By mid-year:** One new sentence pattern per week
- **By winter break:** Students could communicate in English

Genki English uses:
- Simple, catchy songs with repetitive patterns
- Game-based practice in pairs and groups
- Graded vocabulary (not overwhelming)
- Themes children actually want to talk about

### TPR for Classroom Language

Total Physical Response was used from day one to build receptive language:

**Core TPR commands:**
- Stand up, sit down
- Walk walk, run run run
- Stop, go
- Go to sleep, wake up wake up
- Turn around, clap your hands
- Touch your (nose/ears/head)
- Open your book, close your book
- Look at me, listen

**Why it works:** Students respond physically without needing to speak. This builds comprehension before production. Movement makes language memorable. The classroom becomes active and joyful.

**Marie's approach:** "We had SO much fun. I brought that back from the UAE to the US and taught my Kindergarteners. All my English Learners were doing well in my class and many times were sometimes ahead of my English native students."

### Protecting the Home Language

> **Critical Warning (Wong Fillmore, UC Berkeley):** Children can lose their home language within 2-3 years of English-only schooling. This damages family communication, cultural identity, and the cognitive advantages of bilingualism.

**What to do:**
- NEVER tell parents to "speak only English at home"
- Encourage parents to read to children in their home language
- Allow students to discuss with partners in their home language during reading activities (this IS translanguaging, and it deepens comprehension)
- Include bilingual books in the classroom library
- Celebrate multilingualism: "How many languages do we speak in our class?"
- Frame bilingualism as a path to the **Seal of Biliteracy** (a state-recognized diploma credential in 49+ states)

### Why ELLs Sometimes Outpace Native Speakers

The structured, systematic approach actually gives ELLs an advantage:
- They get explicit phonics instruction from the start (native speakers sometimes have gaps filled by assumed knowledge)
- Genki English and TPR build oral language alongside decoding (dual support)
- The multi-sensory approach (movement, song, visual, auditory) works for ALL learners
- Small group instruction means personalized support

## Comprehension Strategies

> **Biliteracy Connection (Snow, Carlo):** Catherine Snow's research at Harvard shows that oral language proficiency in BOTH languages predicts reading comprehension from grade 3 onward. Maria Carlo's research shows that explicitly teaching cognates (family/familia, important/importante) accelerates vocabulary growth for bilingual students. During comprehension activities, use translanguaging: let students discuss in their home language first, then produce in English. This deepens comprehension rather than limiting it.

### When Comprehension Instruction Begins

Comprehension is secondary UNTIL beginning reading is established. This is deliberate prioritization, not neglect. You cannot comprehend text you cannot decode.

Once students can decode with reasonable fluency, comprehension strategies are taught explicitly:

### Strategy 1: Main Idea & Details (The Puzzle Technique)

Marie's signature technique, field-tested in UAE and US with strong results for ELLs.

**How it works:**
1. Show students individual puzzle pieces. Each piece shows one detail (one character doing one thing).
2. Students examine each piece and describe what they see (supporting details).
3. Pieces are assembled on the board, one at a time.
4. When the full picture is assembled, students identify what the whole scene is about (main idea).
5. Students use the pieces as a graphic organizer for writing.

**Example:** A community soccer game puzzle:
- Piece 1: A girl kicking a soccer ball
- Piece 2: A boy cheering in the crowd
- Piece 3: A woman as the goalie blocking a shot
- Piece 4: A man as the referee blowing a whistle
- Piece 5: A child as the water carrier

**When assembled:** Everyone is at a soccer game. Main idea: A community soccer game.

**Why this works for ELLs:** The visual pieces make abstract concepts (main idea vs. details) concrete and visible. Students can describe what they see before they need to read.

### Strategy 2: B-M-E (Beginning-Middle-End)

From Jan Richardson's framework (Levels F-I):
- Students write one sentence about what happened at the beginning
- One sentence about what happened in the middle
- One sentence about how it ended

### Strategy 3: S-W-B-S (Somebody-Wanted-But-So)

From Jan Richardson's framework (Levels G-I):
- **Somebody**: Who is the story about?
- **Wanted**: What did this character want?
- **But**: What problem or obstacle occurred?
- **So**: How did it end?

### Strategy 4: KWL Charts

- **K**: What do I already Know about this topic?
- **W**: What do I Want to learn?
- **L**: What did I Learn?

Filled in progressively as students read informational text.

### Strategy 5: Compare and Contrast

Using paired texts or cross-cultural story variants (e.g., different Cinderella stories from around the world). Venn diagrams to organize similarities and differences.

### Strategy 6: Fact vs. Opinion

Students sort statements from informational text into Fact or Opinion categories.

### Strategy 7: Asking Questions

Students generate Who/What/Where/When/Why/How questions about a text before, during, and after reading.

## Research Base

This framework draws from and aligns with:

**Science of Reading:**
- **National Reading Panel** (2000): All five pillars explicitly addressed
- **Scarborough's Reading Rope** (2001): Both word recognition and language comprehension strands developed
- **Ehri's Phases of Word Reading**: Automaticity cards move students through partial to full to consolidated alphabetic phases
- **Jolly Phonics research**: Independent studies show 12-month reading age advantage after one year of instruction
- **Jan Richardson's research**: RISE intervention data shows 3x expected reading gains across 1,814 students
- **TPR research (Asher)**: Reduced anxiety, better vocabulary retention, higher engagement
- **Genki English**: Aligned with comprehensible input theory (Krashen) and communicative language teaching

**Biliteracy & ELL Research:**
- **Cummins (BICS/CALP, CUP)**: Academic English takes 5-7 years. Literacy skills transfer across languages through shared cognitive foundations. Name phonics and identity affirmation are prerequisites for language learning.
- **Krashen (Comprehensible Input, Affective Filter)**: Language acquired through meaningful input at i+1. Low-anxiety environments (songs, games, movement) accelerate acquisition.
- **Garcia (Translanguaging)**: Bilinguals have one integrated system. Allowing home language use during reading activities deepens comprehension.
- **Escamilla (Literacy Squared, "The Bridge")**: Simultaneous biliteracy from Kindergarten. Explicit cross-language connections accelerate both languages.
- **Hornberger (Continua of Biliteracy)**: Build from oral, contextualized, minority language toward written, decontextualized, majority language.
- **Collier & Thomas (Prism Model)**: Only dual language programs fully close the achievement gap. All four dimensions (sociocultural, linguistic, academic, cognitive) must be addressed.
- **Goldenberg (ELL Research Synthesis)**: Good mainstream instruction + explicit vocabulary + oral language + L1 support.
- **Carlo (Cognate Awareness)**: Explicitly teach cognates to accelerate vocabulary growth for bilingual students.
- **Wong Fillmore (Language Loss)**: Protect home language. English-only schooling causes L1 loss in 2-3 years.
- **SIOP, GLAD, WIDA, CA ELD Framework**: Operational instructional frameworks used in K-3 classrooms nationwide.
